Industry Briefing

About Asford Materials

Know the market before you move.

What We Make

Asford Materials manufactures precast concrete infrastructure products — the unglamorous backbone of American civil construction. Every road drainage system, every municipal sewer junction, every highway culvert is built from products like ours.

  • RCP Pipe — Reinforced Concrete Pipe, 12–96 inch diameter. Manufactured to ASTM C76. The workhorse product. Storm drainage, culverts, sanitary sewer. DOT-spec. Bid-driven, volume-sensitive.
  • Manholes & Junction Boxes — Precast concrete access structures. Municipal utility work. Repeat customers. Relationship-driven sales.
  • Inlets & End Treatments — Curb inlets, flared end sections, headwalls. Smaller-ticket, higher-margin. Often bundled with pipe orders.

The Precast Concrete Market

$170B global 5.6–6.3% CAGR US = largest market

The global precast concrete market is valued at approximately $170 billion and is growing at a compound annual rate of 5.6–6.3%, driven by infrastructure investment, urbanization, and the accelerating replacement of aging water and sewer systems across the United States.

The US market is the single largest geography. Federal infrastructure spending — IIJA, state DOT budgets, municipal bond issuance — is the primary demand driver. Precast is not discretionary. When a road washes out, the pipe gets replaced. When a city expands, manholes get installed. Demand is sticky, cyclical only at the margins, and structurally growing.

Regional producers like Asford compete on delivery radius, relationship depth, and price. Concrete is heavy. Freight economics limit how far a plant can ship. A Birmingham plant serves Alabama, parts of Tennessee, Georgia, and Mississippi. That's the moat — and the ceiling.

Private Equity in Precast

The precast concrete industry is in the middle of a PE-driven consolidation wave. The thesis is simple: the market is fragmented, demand is infrastructure-linked and non-cyclical, margins are predictable, and regional operators are often family-owned with no succession plan and aging equipment.

KPS Capital Partners has assembled a platform of 202 facilities with $19.5B in AUM. They are not buying companies — they are building a national infrastructure manufacturing monopoly, plant by plant. Their playbook: acquire a regional operator, install professional management, cut costs, add capacity, and use the platform to underbid independents on large DOT contracts.

TJC (The Jordan Company) and other mid-market PE firms are running similar strategies at smaller scale. The pattern is consistent: identify a fragmented market with recurring demand, find the family-owned operators who are tired or succession-challenged, and roll them up.

PE threat is existential.

A PE-backed competitor with 20 plants and a $500M credit facility can price below your cost of capital on a DOT bid and absorb the loss. They are not trying to win that bid — they are trying to make you unprofitable until you sell. If you do not grow, professionalize, and build a defensible position in the next five years, you will either be acquired on their terms or competed into irrelevance.
